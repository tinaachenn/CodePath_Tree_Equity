Learning More

In many major cities in the US, there are disparities in trees depending on a neighborhood's socioeconomic status. For instance, Baltimore's TreeBaltimore initiative found that some neighborhoods had as little as 10-20% tree canopy cover, while others had over 50%. Philadelphia's analysis showed that low-income areas had up to 22% less tree cover compared to wealthier neighborhoods. In Phoenix, Central City's tree canopy coverage is only 6.1% while the more affluent Paradise Valley is at triple the amount - 18.2%

Cities benefit far more economically, socially, and recreationally than they do with the removal of trees. As more and more trees are being removed around the globe and in cities, replenishing the world's supply is more critical than ever especially with the looming threats of climate change. Trees are one of the easier and more resource rich methods of tackling climate change and global warming. Today's cities simply do not have enough trees.

For more data: American Forests Research and Reports


Why Tree Equity Matters

  1. Environmental Justice: Historically, marginalized communities have often borne a disproportionate burden of environmental challenges, including lack of green spaces and tree cover. Tree equity is a step towards rectifying these inequities.
  2. Health Benefits: Trees improve air quality, reduce temperatures, and provide spaces for physical activity and mental relaxation. Ensuring tree equity means that all communities can enjoy these health benefits.
  3. Economic Benefits: Trees increase property values and attract businesses. By ensuring that all neighborhoods have sufficient tree cover, you promote economic stability and growth.
  4. Climate Resilience: Trees play a crucial role in mitigating the effects of climate change. They help absorb carbon dioxide, reduce energy consumption, and provide natural cooling in urban areas. Making sure every community has access to these benefits is vital for climate resilience.
  5. Community Well-Being: Trees contribute to a sense of community, provide spaces for social interaction, and improve overall quality of life. When communities have equitable access to trees, they tend to be more vibrant and cohesive.
  6. Educational Opportunities: Trees can serve as outdoor classrooms, providing opportunities for environmental education and experiential learning. All communities should have access to these educational resources.
  7. Wildlife Habitat: Trees create habitats for various species, promoting biodiversity. Tree equity ensures that wildlife can thrive in urban environments, benefiting both the environment and local ecosystems.
  8. Psychological Benefits: Studies have shown that exposure to nature and green spaces has positive effects on mental health and well-being. Tree equity is an important factor in promoting mental health in communities.
  9. Public Safety: Trees help to reduce crime rates and create safer, more inviting public spaces. Allowing all communities to benefit from this effect contributes to safer neighborhoods.
  10. Resilience to Natural Disasters: Trees can provide protection against natural disasters like floods, storms, and landslides. Ensuring that vulnerable communities have access to these natural defenses is crucial for their safety and resilience.
